Forces and Equilibrium

A force is any interaction that, when unopposed, will change the motion of an object. Forces are vector quantities with magnitude, direction, and point of application.

\[ \vec{F} = m\vec{a} \] (Newton's Second Law)

Vector Representation of Forces

20°

Figure 1: Vector representation of a 50 N force at 20° to horizontal

Equilibrium Conditions

For a body to be in static equilibrium, two conditions must be satisfied:

\[ \sum \vec{F} = 0 \] (Sum of all forces equals zero)

\[ \sum \vec{M} = 0 \] (Sum of all moments equals zero)

Example: Equilibrium of Concurrent Forces

Three forces act on a point: F₁ = 100 N at 0°, F₂ = 150 N at 120°, and F₃ = ? at 240°. Find F₃ for equilibrium.

Step 1: Resolve forces into components

F₁: F₁ₓ = 100 N, F₁ᵧ = 0 N

F₂: F₂ₓ = 150 cos 120° = -75 N, F₂ᵧ = 150 sin 120° = 129.9 N

Step 2: Apply equilibrium conditions

ΣFₓ = 0: 100 - 75 + F₃ₓ = 0 → F₃ₓ = -25 N

ΣFᵧ = 0: 0 + 129.9 + F₃ᵧ = 0 → F₃ᵧ = -129.9 N

Step 3: Calculate magnitude and direction

F₃ = √((-25)² + (-129.9)²) = √(625 + 16874) = √17599 = 132.7 N

θ = tan⁻¹(-129.9/-25) = 79.1° + 180° = 259.1° (in third quadrant)

Stress and Strain

Stress is defined as force per unit area, while strain is the deformation per unit length.

\[ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} \] (Normal stress)

\[ \tau = \frac{F}{A} \] (Shear stress)

\[ \epsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L} \] (Normal strain)

\[ \gamma = \frac{\Delta x}{L} \] (Shear strain)

Hooke's Law

For many materials, stress is proportional to strain in the elastic region:

\[ \sigma = E\epsilon \]

Where E is the Young's modulus or modulus of elasticity.

Key Concepts in Mechanics

  • Newton's First Law: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a net external force
  • Newton's Second Law: F = ma (Force equals mass times acceleration)
  • Newton's Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction
  • Stress (σ): Force per unit area (σ = F/A)
  • Strain (ε): Deformation per unit length (ε = ΔL/L)
  • Young's Modulus (E): Ratio of stress to strain in elastic deformation (E = σ/ε)

Stress-Strain Relationships

\[ \sigma = E\epsilon \] (Hooke's Law for elastic deformation)

\[ \tau = G\gamma \] (Shear stress-strain relationship)

\[ \nu = -\frac{\epsilon_{lateral}}{\epsilon_{axial}} \] (Poisson's Ratio)

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law states that the current through a conductor between two points is directly proportional to the voltage across the two points and inversely proportional to the resistance between them.

\[ V = IR \]

Where:

V = Voltage (volts, V)

I = Current (amperes, A)

R = Resistance (ohms, Ω)

Ohm's Law Triangle

R

V = I × R

I
V

I = V / R

R
V

R = V / I

Example: Calculating Current

A circuit has a resistance of 100 Ω and a voltage of 12 V. Calculate the current flowing through the circuit.

Step 1: Apply Ohm's Law

\[ I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{12}{100} = 0.12 \, \text{A} \]

Step 2: Convert to milliamps if needed

\[ I = 0.12 \, \text{A} = 120 \, \text{mA} \]

Kirchhoff's Laws

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL) states that the algebraic sum of currents entering a node (or junction) is zero.

\[ \sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} \]

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL) states that the algebraic sum of all voltages around any closed loop in a circuit is zero.

\[ \sum V = 0 \]

Kirchhoff's Laws Application

Circuit with multiple loops and nodes

KCL: I₁ = I₂ + I₃ at node A

KVL: V₁ - V₂ - V₃ = 0 in loop 1

Circuit Analysis Steps

  1. Identify all nodes and loops in the circuit
  2. Assign current directions (arbitrary - if wrong, the answer will be negative)
  3. Apply KCL at each node (except one)
  4. Apply KVL for each independent loop
  5. Solve the resulting system of equations

Key Electrical Formulas

  • Ohm's Law: V = IR
  • Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
  • Resistors in Series: Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + ... + Rₙ
  • Resistors in Parallel: 1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ... + 1/Rₙ
  • Capacitance: C = Q/V
  • Inductive Reactance: XL = 2πfL
  • Capacitive Reactance: XC = 1/(2πfC)
  • Impedance: Z = √(R² + (XL - XC)²)

AC Circuit Formulas

\[ V_{RMS} = \frac{V_{peak}}{\sqrt{2}} \]

\[ I_{RMS} = \frac{I_{peak}}{\sqrt{2}} \]

\[ P_{avg} = V_{RMS} I_{RMS} \cos \phi \] (Real power)

\[ S = V_{RMS} I_{RMS} \] (Apparent power)

\[ Q = V_{RMS} I_{RMS} \sin \phi \] (Reactive power)

First Law of Thermodynamics

The First Law of Thermodynamics is a statement of conservation of energy for thermodynamic systems.

\[ \Delta U = Q - W \]

Where:

ΔU = Change in internal energy of the system

Q = Heat transferred to the system

W = Work done by the system

Sign Convention

  • Heat added to the system: Q > 0
  • Heat removed from the system: Q < 0
  • Work done by the system: W > 0
  • Work done on the system: W < 0

Example: First Law Application

A system receives 500 J of heat and does 200 J of work on its surroundings. What is the change in internal energy?

Step 1: Identify known values

Q = +500 J (heat added to system)

W = +200 J (work done by system)

Step 2: Apply First Law

\[ \Delta U = Q - W = 500 - 200 = 300 \, \text{J} \]

Step 3: Interpret result

The internal energy of the system increases by 300 J

Ideal Gas Law

The Ideal Gas Law describes the relationship between pressure, volume, and temperature for an ideal gas.

\[ PV = nRT \]

Where:

P = Pressure (Pa)

V = Volume (m³)

n = Number of moles

R = Universal gas constant = 8.314 J/(mol·K)

T = Temperature (K)

Common Forms of Ideal Gas Law

\[ PV = mRT \] (Using specific gas constant R)

\[ \frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2} \] (For constant mass)

\[ P = \rho RT \] (Using density ρ)

Example: Gas Compression

A gas occupies 2 m³ at 300 K and 100 kPa. If it is compressed to 1 m³ and heated to 400 K, what is the new pressure?

Step 1: Apply combined gas law

\[ \frac{P_1 V_1}{T_1} = \frac{P_2 V_2}{T_2} \]

Step 2: Rearrange for P₂

\[ P_2 = P_1 \times \frac{V_1}{V_2} \times \frac{T_2}{T_1} \]

Step 3: Substitute values

\[ P_2 = 100 \times \frac{2}{1} \times \frac{400}{300} = 100 \times 2 \times 1.333 = 266.7 \, \text{kPa} \]

Thermodynamic Laws

  • Zeroth Law: If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other
  • First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another (ΔU = Q - W)
  • Second Law: The entropy of an isolated system never decreases
  • Third Law: The entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero

Key Thermodynamic Equations

\[ \Delta U = Q - W \] (First Law)

\[ \eta = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} \] (Thermal Efficiency)

\[ \eta_{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} \] (Carnot Efficiency)

\[ PV = nRT \] (Ideal Gas Law)

\[ \Delta S \geq \int \frac{\delta Q}{T} \] (Entropy change)

Hydrostatic Pressure

Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid at equilibrium due to the force of gravity.

\[ P = P_0 + \rho g h \]

Where:

P = Pressure at depth h (Pa)

P₀ = Pressure at surface (Pa)

ρ = Density of fluid (kg/m³)

g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s²)

h = Depth below surface (m)

Pressure Variation with Depth

F = P × A

Surface: P = P₀

↓
F = (P₀ + ρgh) × A

Depth h: P = P₀ + ρgh

Example: Pressure at Depth

Calculate the pressure at a depth of 10 m in water. Atmospheric pressure is 101.3 kPa. Density of water is 1000 kg/m³.

Step 1: Identify known values

P₀ = 101.3 kPa = 101300 Pa

ρ = 1000 kg/m³

g = 9.81 m/s²

h = 10 m

Step 2: Apply hydrostatic pressure formula

\[ P = P_0 + \rho g h \]

\[ P = 101300 + (1000 \times 9.81 \times 10) \]

Step 3: Calculate

\[ P = 101300 + 98100 = 199400 \, \text{Pa} = 199.4 \, \text{kPa} \]

Bernoulli's Equation

Bernoulli's equation describes the relationship between pressure, velocity, and elevation in a flowing fluid.

\[ P_1 + \frac{1}{2} \rho v_1^2 + \rho g h_1 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2} \rho v_2^2 + \rho g h_2 \]

Assumptions for Bernoulli's Equation

  • Steady flow
  • Incompressible fluid
  • Inviscid flow (no friction)
  • Flow along a streamline

Applications of Bernoulli's Principle

  • Venturi effect: Pressure decreases when velocity increases in a constriction
  • Airplane wings: Lift generated by pressure difference above and below wing
  • Carburetors: Fuel drawn in by low pressure in venturi
  • Pitot tubes: Measure flow velocity using pressure difference

Fundamental Fluid Principles

  • Continuity Equation: A₁V₁ = A₂V₂ (for incompressible flow)
  • Bernoulli's Equation: P₁ + ½ρV₁² + ρgz₁ = P₂ + ½ρV₂² + ρgz₂
  • Hydrostatic Pressure: P = P₀ + ρgh
  • Reynolds Number: Re = ρVD/μ (indicates flow regime)
  • Darcy-Weisbach Equation: hf = f(L/D)(V²/2g) (head loss in pipes)

Fluid Flow Equations

\[ \dot{m} = \rho A V \] (Mass flow rate)

\[ Q = A V \] (Volumetric flow rate)

\[ \frac{P_1}{\rho g} + \frac{V_1^2}{2g} + z_1 = \frac{P_2}{\rho g} + \frac{V_2^2}{2g} + z_2 + h_L \] (Energy equation with losses)

\[ Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu} \] (Reynolds number)

Practice Problems

Problem 1
Stress Analysis

A steel rod with a diameter of 20 mm is subjected to a tensile force of 50 kN. Calculate the stress in the rod.

Given Data:

Diameter (d) 20 mm
Force (F) 50 kN

Step 1: Calculate Cross-sectional Area

\[ A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4} = \frac{\pi (0.02)^2}{4} \]

\[ A = \frac{\pi \times 0.0004}{4} = 3.1416 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m}^2 \]

Step 2: Calculate Stress

\[ \sigma = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{50 \times 10^3}{3.1416 \times 10^{-4}} \]

\[ \sigma = 159.15 \times 10^6 \, \text{Pa} = 159.15 \, \text{MPa} \]

Solution

σ = 159.15 MPa
Problem 2
Circuit Analysis

For the circuit shown below, calculate the total resistance and current through each resistor when a 12V supply is connected.

Given Data:

R₁ 10 Ω
R₂ 20 Ω
R₃ 30 Ω
Supply Voltage 12 V

Step 1: Calculate Total Resistance

For resistors in series: RT = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

\[ R_T = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60 \, \Omega \]

Step 2: Calculate Total Current

Using Ohm's Law: I = V/R

\[ I = \frac{12}{60} = 0.2 \, \text{A} \]

Step 3: Calculate Current Through Each Resistor

In a series circuit, current is the same through all components

\[ I_{R1} = I_{R2} = I_{R3} = 0.2 \, \text{A} \]

Solution

RT = 60 Ω
I = 0.2 A
