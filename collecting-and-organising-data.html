Collecting and Organising Data

The Foundation of Engineering Analysis

[cite_start]Statistics is the science of using data to make decisions in the face of uncertainty[cite: 199]. The quality of these decisions depends entirely on the quality of the data collected. This lesson covers the essential principles for gathering, classifying, and organising data effectively.

Planning and Recording Data

[cite_start]The process of observing and recording data should always be planned in advance to ensure quality and reliability[cite: 205]. Hasty or unplanned data collection often leads to inaccurate or unusable results.

Key Steps for Good Quality Measurement

[cite_start]To ensure your data is reliable, consider the following points before you begin[cite: 235]:
    [cite_start]
  • Plan Ahead: Design your observation sheet beforehand, detailing what you will record, the units, and any relevant conditions[cite: 207, 236].
    • [cite_start]
  • Check the System: Ensure the process or system you are measuring is running normally[cite: 206, 237].
    • [cite_start]
  • Calibrate Instruments: Verify that all measurement instruments are properly calibrated and operating correctly[cite: 206, 238, 239].
    • [cite_start]
  • Record Raw Data Directly: Write down exactly what you observe without performing 'on-the-fly' calculations or conversions[cite: 212, 241].
    • [cite_start]
  • Note Start and End Conditions: Record the state of instruments and systems at the beginning and end of the observation period to identify any significant changes[cite: 210, 214, 242].

Accuracy vs. Precision

[cite_start]In engineering, it's vital to understand the difference between accuracy and precision[cite: 220].

    [cite_start]
  • Accuracy is how close a measurement is to the true, actual value[cite: 221].
    • [cite_start]
  • Precision is how close a series of measurements are to each other, indicating consistency or low variation[cite: 221].
[cite_start]The goal is to achieve measurements that are both accurate and precise[cite: 223].

Types of Data

[cite_start]Data can be broadly classified into two main types: qualitative and quantitative[cite: 249].

Qualitative Data

Describes qualities or characteristics that cannot be measured numerically or arranged in a logical order. [cite_start]Examples include colour, shape, or fault type[cite: 250].

Quantitative Data

Represents quantities and can be measured numerically. [cite_start]This is the most common type of data in engineering and is further divided into two categories[cite: 251].

Discrete vs. Continuous Data

[cite_start]Quantitative data can be either discrete (counted) or continuous (measured)[cite: 251].

Discrete Data

Can only take specific, separate integer values. [cite_start]It is typically data that is counted[cite: 251, 252].

Examples:

    [cite_start]
  • Number of dents in a surface [cite: 265, 267]
    • [cite_start]
  • Wire breaks in a process [cite: 285, 287]
    • [cite_start]
  • Number of undersize washers in a batch [cite: 437]

Continuous Data

Can take any value within a given range. [cite_start]It is typically data that is measured[cite: 251, 254].

Examples:

    [cite_start]
  • Length of a heater wire [cite: 257, 259]
    • [cite_start]
  • Diameter of a wire [cite: 269, 271]
    • [cite_start]
  • Temperature of coolant [cite: 277, 279]

Organising Raw Data

[cite_start]Once data is collected, it needs to be organised to reveal patterns and trends[cite: 164]. [cite_start]A tally chart is often the first step in summarising raw data, especially for producing frequency diagrams[cite: 433].

Example: Creating a Tally Chart for Discrete Data

[cite_start]

Consider the number of undersize washers recorded each hour over a 48-hour period[cite: 437]:
[cite_start]3, 5, 8, 4, 5, 1, 3, 7, 9, 6, 7, 6, 2, 5, 10, 8, 9, 7, 4, 6, 2, 5, 1, 5, 4, 0, 2, 6, 8, 5, 7, 3, 5, 1, 9, 6, 3, 5, 3, 9, 2, 1, 8, 2, 9, 5, 10, 4 [cite: 438, 439, 442]

Steps to Organise:

    [cite_start]
  1. Find the Range: Scan the data to find the minimum (0) and maximum (10) values[cite: 444, 445]. [cite_start]The range is 10 - 0 = 10[cite: 447].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Define Classes: For this discrete data, we can use a class width of 1, giving 11 classes (0 through 10)[cite: 452].
  3. Tally the Data: Go through the data and make a tally mark for each value in its corresponding class. [cite_start]Occurrences are often grouped in fives for easy counting[cite: 453, 455].
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Count Frequencies: Sum the tally marks to get the frequency for each class[cite: 456].
